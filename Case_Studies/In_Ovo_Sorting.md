### 1. The Industrial and Regulatory Friction

In high-throughput industrial processes, companies are increasingly confronted with hard biological and regulatory barriers. A prime example is the European regulatory shift banning the crushing of male day-old chicks, forcing the poultry industry to sort embryos *in-ovo* (inside the egg) prior to the threshold of sensory perception (typically before the 9th day of incubation).

The physical constraints are extreme:
*   **Structural Integrity (The Shell)**: The shell must remain intact. Any perforation or drilling introduces risk of contamination and compromises embryological development.
*   **Extreme Velocity (Flow)**: The industrial line must process a minimum flow of **100,000 eggs per hour**.
*   **Sensor Limitations**: Standard research departments often suffer from the **"Monotheism of the Sensor"**—the obsessive search for a single, perfect sensor (e.g., MRI or hyperspectral imaging) that would resolve the sorting with 100% accuracy. FASCIA diagnostics demonstrate that such a single-point solution is either economically unviable or statistically insufficient under massive flow conditions.

### 3. The Fusion Principle: Why Stacking Works

If an auditor relies on a single sensor layer, the error margin is too high for industrial viability:
*   Let the error rate of Layer 1 be $P(E_1) = 0.05$.
*   Let the error rate of Layer 2 be $P(E_2) = 0.08$.
*   Let the error rate of Layer 3 be $P(E_3) = 0.10$.

In isolation, each sensor produces unacceptable rates of false positives or false negatives. However, by designing a layered audit matrix, the probability of simultaneous error across all three independent layers drops exponentially:

$$P(\text{Global Error}) = P(E_1) \times P(E_2) \times P(E_3) = 0.05 \times 0.08 \times 0.10 = 0.0004 \text{ (or } 0.04\% \text{)}$$

This is the **Loi Tau**: stacking independent, fallible layers of physical detection to construct a sovereign, high-velocity decision boundary.
